Stand on the shoulder of giants¶

  • “Stand on the shoulders of giants. Don’t dig up their bones.” – Sir Isaac Newton

  • Conserving Power - In an evolutionary sense, the ability to work in abstractions allows us to conserve vast amounts of brainpower. Imagine the overwhelm of having to concern yourself with how everything works all of the time, from gravity to your musculoskeletal system, to your heart, the hardware and software stacks of your phone, and so on. We wouldn’t leave our beds. Trust in abstractions and analogies allow us to function. First principles and asking why

  • Favour heuristic or analogy based thinking aka pattern matching over first principles thinking when the problem you are solving requires speed over depth.

  • Respect what came before. Principal Engineering Community Tenets

Magic¶

http://www.catb.org/jargon/html/M/magic.html

  1. (adj.) As yet unexplained, or too complicated to explain; compare automagically and (Arthur C.) Clarke’s Third Law: “Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic.” “TTY echoing is controlled by a large number of magic bits.” “This routine magically computes the parity of an 8-bit byte in three instructions.”

  2. (adj.) Characteristic of something that works although no one really understands why (this is especially called black magic).

  3. (n.) [Stanford] A feature not generally publicized that allows something otherwise impossible, or a feature formerly in that category but now unveiled.

  4. (n.) The ultimate goal of all engineering & development, elegance in the extreme; from the first corollary to Clarke’s Third Law: “Any technology distinguishable from magic is insufficiently advanced”.

I hate fights¶

“I hate fights. You never do your best work in a fight, because fights are not sufficiently general. Winning is always a function of the situation and the people involved. You don’t win fights by thinking of big ideas but by thinking of tricks that work in one particular case. And yet fighting is just as much work as thinking about real problems. Which is particularly painful to someone who cares how their brain is used: your brain goes fast but you get nowhere, like a car spinning its wheels.” – Paul Graham, Mean People Fail

How mathematics was (probably) invented¶

Disclaimer: Scenarios generated by Claude. Might not be factually correct.

  • Arithmetic: Ancient merchant: “I have 2 sheep and trade for 2 more. Now I have 4 sheep.” They developed number symbols and basic addition to count and combine quantities.

  • Algebra: Egyptian scribe: “The Pharaoh wants to know how many blocks are needed to complete the pyramid. If we have 5 layers and need 10 total, how many more do we need?” They created equations with unknowns (e.g., 5 + x = 10) to solve for missing values.

  • Geometry: Babylonian surveyor: “After the flood, we need to reestablish field boundaries. This field is 3 rods by 4 rods. What’s its area?” They derived area formulas (e.g., A = length × width) to calculate land measurements.

  • Calculus: Newton: “To predict the planet’s position, we need to understand how its velocity changes over time.” He invented differential calculus to model rates of change and planetary motion.

  • Statistics: 17th century demographer: “To plan for the future, we need to know the average family size in our city.” They developed methods to calculate means and analyze data distributions.

  • Number Theory: Ancient Greek philosopher: “Is 17 a fundamental building block of numbers that can’t be divided further?” They explored prime numbers and their properties, leading to fundamental theorems.

  • Topology: 19th century mathematician: “Can we classify shapes based on properties that don’t change when the shape is stretched or bent?” They created new axioms to define and study topological invariants.

  • Discrete Math: Early computer scientist: “To optimize this network, we need to represent connections between computers as a graph.” They formalized graph theory to model and analyze network structures mathematically.
