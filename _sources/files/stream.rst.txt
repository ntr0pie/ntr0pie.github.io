**Stand on the shoulder of giants**
"""""""""""""""""""""""""""""""""""
* "*Stand on the shoulders of giants. Don't dig up their bones.*" -- Sir Isaac Newton
* Conserving Power - In an evolutionary sense, the ability to work in abstractions allows us to conserve vast amounts of brainpower. Imagine the overwhelm of having to concern yourself with how everything works all of the time, from gravity to your musculoskeletal system, to your heart, the hardware and software stacks of your phone, and so on. We wouldn’t leave our beds. Trust in abstractions and analogies allow us to function. `First principles and asking why <https://www.theengineeringmanager.com/growth/first-principles-and-asking-why/>`_
* Favour heuristic or analogy based thinking aka pattern matching over first principles thinking when the problem you are solving requires speed over depth. 
* Respect what came before. `Principal Engineering Community Tenets <https://www.amazon.jobs/en/landing_pages/pe-community-tenets>`_

#. (adj.) As yet unexplained, or too complicated to explain; compare automagically and (Arthur C.) Clarke's Third Law: “Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic.” “TTY echoing is controlled by a large number of magic bits.” “This routine magically computes the parity of an 8-bit byte in three instructions.”

#. (adj.) Characteristic of something that works although no one really understands why (this is especially called black magic).

#. (n.) [Stanford] A feature not generally publicized that allows something otherwise impossible, or a feature formerly in that category but now unveiled.

#. (n.) The ultimate goal of all engineering & development, elegance in the extreme; from the first corollary to Clarke's Third Law: “Any technology distinguishable from magic is insufficiently advanced”.

**I hate fights**
----------------------

You don't win fights by thinking of big ideas but by thinking of tricks that work in one particular case. And yet fighting is just as much work as thinking about real problems. **How Mathematics was (probably) invented**
-------------------------------------------
| **Arithmetic | Ancient Mesopotamia, c. 3000 BCE**

* "I have 5 sheep and trade for 3 more. Now I have 8 sheep."
* Early civilizations developed number systems and basic operations to count, trade, and manage resources. Clay tablets from Sumer show early numerical records.

| **Geometry | Ancient Egypt, c. 2000 BCE**

* "The Nile has flooded. We must resurvey the fields. This plot is 10 cubits by 12 cubits. What's its area?"
* Egyptians and Babylonians developed practical geometry for land surveying, construction, and astronomy. The Greeks later formalized geometric proofs.

| **Algebra | Baghdad, 9th century CE**

* Al-Khwarizmi: "If a trader doubles his capital and spends 50 dirhams, he has 20 dirhams left. What was his initial capital?"
* While algebraic thinking existed earlier, Al-Khwarizmi's work formalized algebra as a distinct branch of mathematics.

| **Calculus | England/Germany, late 17th CE**

* Newton: "To predict the planet's position, we need to understand how its velocity changes over time."
* Leibniz: "How can we find the optimal shape for a container to minimize material costs?"
* Newton and Leibniz independently developed calculus to solve problems of motion, optimization, and rates of change.

| **Statistics | England, 17th CE**

* John Graunt: "By analyzing birth and death records, we can predict population growth and plan for the future."
* Early statisticians like Graunt and later Gauss developed methods to analyze data and make predictions, laying the groundwork for modern statistics.

| **Number Theory | Ancient Greece, c. 300 BCE**

* Euclid: "Are there infinitely many prime numbers? How can we prove this?"
* While number theory has ancient roots, Euclid's "Elements" provided rigorous proofs of fundamental theorems, setting the stage for future developments.

| **Topology | Germany, late 19th CE**

* Topology emerged from studies in geometry and analysis, with pioneers like Möbius and Poincaré exploring properties invariant under continuous deformations.

| **Discrete Mathematics | Switzerland, 18th CE**

* Euler: "Is it possible to cross all of Königsberg's bridges without repeating any?"
* Euler's analysis of this problem in 1736 laid the foundation for graph theory, a key part of discrete mathematics. This field later became crucial in computer science.
